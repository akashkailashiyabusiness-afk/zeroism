Salt: A World History

"The Mineral That Shaped Civilization"

Overview

Published in 2002 by Penguin Books, *Salt: A World History* by Mark Kurlansky is a 496-page exploration of how salt—a seemingly mundane mineral—has profoundly influenced human history across millennia. Spanning 26 chapters, Kurlansky, known for *Cod*, weaves a narrative that traces salt’s role in food preservation, trade, warfare, and culture, from ancient China to modern America. With recipes, anecdotes, and historical vignettes, the book argues that salt’s quest drove economic systems, shaped empires, and even sparked revolutions, earning praise as a "glittering" microhistory despite some critique for its sprawling scope.

Part I: Salt’s Ancient Foundations

The Dawn of Salt Use

Kurlansky begins with salt’s prehistoric significance, used as early as 6000 BCE in China for preserving fish. Salt’s ability to extend food’s shelf life made it a cornerstone of settled societies.

  • China’s Saltworks: Boiling brine in clay pots marked an early industry.
  • Egyptian Preservation: Salted fish and meat sustained workers building pyramids.

Salt in Religion and Ritual

Salt held sacred status—used in Jewish offerings, Roman sacrifices, and Christian symbolism (e.g., "salt of the earth"). Its purity and permanence made it a spiritual metaphor.

  • Roman Salaries: Soldiers paid in salt (“salarium”) gave us “salary.”
  • Cultural Echo: Salt’s ritual use persists in modern traditions like Japanese sumo purification.

Part II: Salt and Empire

Salt Trade and Power

Salt became a commodity of immense value, driving trade routes and imperial wealth. The Venetian salt monopoly in the Mediterranean and China’s salt tax funded dynasties.

  • Venice’s Rise: Salt trade financed its naval empire.
  • China’s Monopoly: Salt taxes supported the Han Dynasty’s bureaucracy.

Salt Wars and Revolts

Control over salt sparked conflicts, like the 1480s Salt War in France, and fueled revolutions. Britain’s salt tax in India famously led to Gandhi’s 1930 Salt March.

  • French Gabelle: A hated salt tax incited rebellion.
  • Gandhi’s Protest: A 240-mile march symbolized resistance to colonial rule.

Part III: Salt’s Technological and Culinary Evolution

Preservation and Innovation

Salt’s role in curing meat, fish, and cheese sustained armies and explorers. Techniques evolved from Roman garum (fermented fish sauce) to medieval salt cod industries.

  • Cod Boom: Salted cod fed Europe and fueled Atlantic trade.
  • Butter and Cheese: Salt preservation birthed culinary staples.

Industrial Salt

The 19th century saw salt mined on a massive scale (e.g., Cheshire, England) for chemical industries—producing soda ash, chlorine, and more—shifting its role from food to manufacturing.

  • Chemical Revolution: Salt underpinned industrial growth.
  • Modern Decline: Refrigeration reduced salt’s food preservation role.

Key Insights and Legacy

Salt as Economic Engine

Kurlansky argues salt was a linchpin of economies—more valuable than gold at times—shaping trade, taxation, and labor systems across cultures.

  • Trade Routes: Saharan salt caravans rivaled spice routes.
  • Modern Parallel: Salt’s industrial uses (e.g., de-icing roads) remain economically vital in 2025.

Cultural and Culinary Impact

Salt’s influence extends to language (“worth his salt”), cuisine (e.g., prosciutto, kimchi), and even health debates (e.g., salt intake controversies).

  • Recipes Included: From ancient garum to American salt pork.
  • 2025 Relevance: Salt’s culinary legacy persists, though health campaigns push low-sodium diets.

Reception and Impact

Critics praised *Salt* as a “dazzling” microhistory, with *The New York Times* calling it a “glittering commodity tale.” Some found its breadth—spanning salt mines, recipes, and revolutions—overwhelming, but it’s lauded for making a humble mineral a lens on humanity. It remains a bestseller, influencing food history studies.
