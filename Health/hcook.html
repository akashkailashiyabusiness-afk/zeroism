Cuisine and Empire: Cooking in World History

"A Global Journey Through the Evolution of Culinary Traditions"

Overview

Published in 2013 as part of the *California Studies in Food and Culture* series, *Cuisine and Empire: Cooking in World History* by historian Rachel Laudan is a sweeping exploration of how cuisines have evolved over 20,000 years, shaped by empires, trade, religion, and shifting culinary philosophies. Unlike traditional food histories that focus on national cuisines, Laudan takes a global perspective, tracing the rise and fall of major culinary traditions from ancient grain-based diets to modern industrial food systems. Spanning 488 pages with 68 photographs, 10 maps, and 6 tables, the book argues that cooking is a human construct—like language or architecture—driven by power, culture, and necessity, challenging romanticized agrarian myths.

Key Themes and Structure

Culinary Philosophy and Empire

Laudan introduces "culinary philosophy"—beliefs about health, religion, politics, and society—as the driving force behind new cuisines. She organizes the book chronologically, linking cuisines to empires that spread them via merchants, missionaries, and militaries.

  • Ancient Roots: Early grain cuisines (e.g., barley-wheat in Mesopotamia) emerged with agriculture, around 10,000 BCE.
  • Religious Influence: Buddhism (260 BCE–800 CE) and Islam (800–1650 CE) transformed Asian cuisines with vegetarianism and halal practices.
  • Imperial Spread: The Roman Empire’s wheat-based cuisine and the British Empire’s industrial food shaped global diets.

The Culinary Family Tree

Beneath the diversity of cuisines, Laudan reveals a simpler "culinary family tree" rooted in staple grains (wheat, rice, maize) and cooking techniques (grinding, fermenting, roasting). She maps how these diffused globally, often tied to imperial power.

Major Historical Shifts

Ancient to Medieval Cuisines

The book begins with the mastery of grain cooking (e.g., porridge, bread) around 20,000 years ago, followed by sacrificial cuisines of ancient empires (500 BCE–400 CE) like Persia and Rome, where food reflected status and cosmology.

  • Buddhist Impact: Vegetarian cuisines spread across South and East Asia.
  • Islamic High Cuisine: Rich, spiced dishes emerged in Central and West Asia, influencing Europe via the Crusades.

Modern Industrial Era

From the 18th century, European empires (British, French) and industrial technologies (canning, milling) created "middling cuisines" accessible to the masses, while the 20th century saw American processed foods dominate globally.

Key Insights and Arguments

Laudan challenges contemporary food movements by defending processed food’s role in human progress and questioning nationalistic and agrarian ideals. Her global lens highlights how cuisines are dynamic, not static traditions.

  • Processing as Progress: Techniques like milling and fermentation made food safer and more accessible.
  • Empire’s Role: Cuisines spread not by chance but through conquest and trade—e.g., sugar from Islamic empires, potatoes from the Americas.
  • Myth-Busting: She critiques the romantic view of pre-industrial diets, noting their labor and scarcity.

Reception and Impact

Critics hail *Cuisine and Empire* as a groundbreaking work. The *New York Review of Books* called it "a triumph" in historiography, while *Times Literary Supplement* praised its "miniature masterpieces of cultural history." Food historians like Paul Freedman laud its unique blend of culinary and historical analysis, though some note its dense academic style may challenge casual readers.
