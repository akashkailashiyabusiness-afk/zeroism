The Hungry Empire

"How Britain’s Quest for Food Shaped the Modern World"

Overview

Published in 2017, *The Hungry Empire* by historian Lizzie Collingham (also titled *The Taste of Empire* in the US) is a 400-page exploration of how Britain’s pursuit of food over 450 years built its empire and reshaped global diets. Structured around 20 meals—from cod stew on Henry VIII’s *Mary Rose* in 1545 to an iguana curry in 1993 Guyana—Collingham reveals how the British Empire wove a global food network, moving plants, people, and cuisines across continents. Winner of the 2018 Guild of Food Writers Book Award, it argues that food was not just sustenance but a driver of imperialism, trade, and cultural exchange, with lasting echoes in 2025’s food systems.

Structure and Key Themes

Twenty Meals as Historical Lens

Each of the 20 chapters opens with a meal—some real, others fictional (e.g., from Dickens)—to anchor a narrative spanning the 16th to 20th centuries. These vignettes trace Britain’s food quest from Newfoundland fisheries to Kenyan farms, blending politics, agriculture, and trade.

  • Cod in Newfoundland (1545): Early fishing ventures kickstarted imperial expansion.
  • Jollof Rice in West Africa (1686): Highlights the slave trade’s food exchanges.
  • Plum Pudding in Britain (1940s): Symbolizes empire-wide ingredients during WWII.

Food as Imperial Power

Collingham posits that Britain’s hunger for cod, sugar, tea, and wheat drove colonization, slavery, and global trade, reshaping landscapes and diets worldwide.

  • Modern Link: In 2025, global supply chains (e.g., Canadian wheat, New Zealand lamb) trace back to this network.

Major Historical Arcs

16th–17th Centuries: Foundations

Britain’s empire began with cod fishing off Newfoundland, followed by sugar plantations in the Caribbean, fueled by enslaved labor. These early ventures established food as a colonial cornerstone.

  • Sugar Boom: By 1700, Caribbean sugar sweetened British tea, tying colonies to the homeland.
  • 2025 Echo: Sugar’s dominance persists in processed foods, sparking health debates.

18th–19th Centuries: Expansion

The empire globalized diets—tea from China via opium trades, rice from West Africa to America, wheat from Canada—while industrializing food production for urban workers.

  • Opium Wars: Secured tea for Britain, spreading the habit empire-wide.
  • Settler Colonies: New Zealand lamb and Australian canned goods fed Britain by the 1900s.

20th Century: Dependency and Legacy

WWII exposed Britain’s reliance on imperial food (e.g., Kenyan maize, Indian tea), while post-war shifts saw colonies adapt British tastes into national cuisines.

  • Bengal Famine (1943): A dark cost of imperial neglect.
  • Modern Parallel: In 2025, UK food security (61% self-sufficiency) reflects this historical outsourcing.

Key Insights

Empire’s Food Web

Collingham shows how Britain harnessed global resources—cod, spices, sugar—displacing native peoples and altering ecosystems, a blueprint for today’s food industry.

  • Columbian Exchange: Maize and potatoes reshaped Africa and Europe.
  • 2025 Relevance: Corporate giants like Tesco echo this imperial reach.

Dark Side of Empire

The book doesn’t shy from slavery, famines, and land theft, though some critics note a lighter touch on colonized perspectives.

  • Slavery’s Role: Millions enslaved for sugar and rice plantations.
  • Current Debate: In 2025, reparations and ethical sourcing remain contentious.

Reception and Modern Connections

Hailed as “dazzling” (*Guardian*) and “wholly pleasing” (*Sunday Times*), *The Hungry Empire* won acclaim for its innovative meal-based structure. Critics like *The Hindu* note its awe at imperial feats can overshadow slavery’s brutality. In 2025, its themes resonate with Brexit-era food security debates, climate-driven supply chain shifts, and the rise of sustainable diets—tracing modern eating habits to Britain’s imperial appetite.
